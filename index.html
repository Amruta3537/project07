Introduction to Medical Plastic Wastage

Medical plastic waste is a significant component of biomedical waste generated in healthcare facilities. This includes a wide range of plastic products such as syringes, intravenous bags, and sterilization wraps. Proper management and recycling of this waste are essential to minimize environmental impact.

Types of Biomedical Waste

The World Health Organization (WHO) categorizes biomedical waste into several types:
  • Infectious Waste: Waste contaminated with blood and other bodily fluids.
  • Sharps: Items that can cause cuts or punctures (e.g., needles, scalpels).
  • Pathological Waste: Human tissues, organs, and fluids.
  • Pharmaceutical Waste: Expired or unused medications.
  • Genotoxic Waste: Highly hazardous, including cytotoxic drugs.
  • Radioactive Waste: Waste containing radioactive substances.
  • Chemical Waste: Discarded chemicals (e.g., solvents, disinfectants).
  • General Waste: Non-hazardous waste comparable to domestic waste.

Average Composition of Hospital Waste

Hospital waste consists of various materials:
  • Bandages, linen, and other infectious waste: 30-35%
  • Plastics: 7-10%
  • Disposable syringes: 0.3-0.5%
  • Glass: 3-5%
  • General wastes including food: 40-45%

Can Medical Plastic Waste be Recycled?

Yes, medical plastic waste can be recycled, especially if it is collected before contamination. For instance, sterilization wraps made of polypropylene can be recycled efficiently if collected before use. Proper segregation and recycling can reduce the environmental impact significantly.

The Plastic Recycling Process Steps

1. Collection + Distribution

The first step involves collecting post-consumer materials from various sources, which can be done by local governments or private companies.

2. Sorting + Categorizing

Sorting is crucial as different types of plastic need to be separated. This is usually done by machines at recycling plants.

3. Washing

Washing removes impurities like labels, adhesives, dirt, and food residue, which is essential for quality recycling.

4. Shredding

Shredding breaks down plastics into smaller pieces, which can be used for other applications without further processing.

5. Identification and Separation of Plastics

Plastic pieces are tested and segregated based on density and quality to ensure proper recycling.

6. Extruding + Compounding

The final step transforms shredded plastic particles into a usable product for manufacturers.
